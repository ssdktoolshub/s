📚 Clinical Pathology Notes: CEA

What is Carcinoembryonic Antigen (CEA)?
CEA is a glycoprotein involved in cell adhesion. It is normally produced in gastrointestinal tissue during fetal development, but production stops or decreases significantly before birth. In adults, it acts as a tumor marker, particularly for colorectal, pancreatic, gastric, lung, and breast cancers.

Why do smokers have higher normal baseline CEA levels?
Smoking induces chronic, low-grade inflammatory changes in the lung and bronchial tissues. This stimulates the local release of CEA, raising the baseline normal limit to 5.0 ng/mL in healthy smokers, compared to 3.0 ng/mL in non-smokers.
