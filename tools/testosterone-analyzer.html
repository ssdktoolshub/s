📚 Clinical Pathology Notes: Testosterone

What is Testosterone?
Testosterone is the primary androgenic sex hormone. In men, it is mostly synthesized by Leydig cells in the testes. In women, it is produced in smaller quantities by the ovaries and adrenal glands. It is responsible for male characteristics, muscle/bone mass, mood, and libido.

Why do reference ranges differ so widely?
Men require substantially higher testosterone levels (300 to 1000 ng/dL) to support spermatogenesis, muscle mass, and virilization. Women exhibit much lower levels (15 to 70 ng/dL); elevations in women are often clinically significant and indicate androgen excess.

What causes abnormal testosterone?
Low testosterone in men (hypogonadism) can stem from primary testicular failure or secondary pituitary deficiency (low LH/FSH), aging, or chronic obesity. High levels in women are most commonly associated with Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS), congenital adrenal hyperplasia, or ovarian tumors.
