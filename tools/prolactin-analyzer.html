📚 Clinical Pathology Notes: Prolactin

What is Prolactin?
Prolactin is a peptide hormone synthesized and secreted by the lactotroph cells of the anterior pituitary gland. Its primary physiological role is to stimulate and sustain lactation (milk production) post-pregnancy, but it also influences reproductive and immune functions.

What is Hyperprolactinemia?
Hyperprolactinemia refers to abnormally high levels of prolactin in the blood. It can suppress hypothalamic GnRH, leading to reduced LH and FSH levels. This results in irregular periods and galactorrhea in women, and erectile dysfunction or decreased libido in men.

What does a value > 100 ng/mL imply?
While mild elevations (30 to 100 ng/dL) are often caused by stress, physical exercise, or medications (e.g. antipsychotics, dopamine antagonists), highly elevated levels (> 100 ng/mL) raise strong suspicion of a prolactinoma (a benign pituitary tumor). Levels above 250 ng/mL are almost always diagnostic of a macroprolactinoma.
