📚 Clinical Pathology Notes: Fibrinogen

What is Fibrinogen?
Fibrinogen (also known as Clotting Factor I) is a soluble plasma glycoprotein synthesized by the liver. During the final stage of the coagulation cascade, thrombin converts fibrinogen into insoluble fibrin strands, which crosslink to form a stable blood clot.

Why are high levels significant?
Fibrinogen is an acute-phase reactant, meaning its blood concentration increases significantly in response to tissue inflammation, trauma, pregnancy, or infections. Chronically elevated fibrinogen increases the risk of cardiovascular events and thrombosis.
